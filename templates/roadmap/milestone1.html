Trading Fundamentals

Understand the basics of trading and set the foundation for your journey as a trader.


What is Trading?

Trading refers to the act of buying and selling financial instruments, such as stocks, forex, or cryptocurrencies, with the intention of making a profit. Traders typically capitalize on short-term price fluctuations in markets. Unlike long-term investing, which is focused on asset growth, trading seeks to benefit from the volatility and daily changes in the market.

  • Day Trading: Involves buying and selling financial instruments within the same day. Day traders aim to capitalize on small price movements in a single trading day and avoid overnight risk. This style requires significant time, attention, and quick decision-making.

  • Swing Trading: Swing traders hold their positions for several days or weeks. They aim to profit from short- to medium-term price movements in the market. Swing trading is less intense than day trading but still requires technical analysis to make informed decisions.

  • Investing: Involves purchasing financial assets with the expectation that their value will grow over time. Unlike trading, investing typically takes a long-term approach. Investors aim to profit from the value appreciation of assets over months or years, rather than quick price movements.

Types of Markets

There are several types of markets where you can trade. Each market operates differently, with its own set of rules, trading hours, and volatility.

  • Stocks: Stocks represent shares of ownership in a publicly traded company. Investors in the stock market are essentially purchasing a small portion of a company’s value. Stock prices can be volatile, influenced by company performance, market conditions, and external factors.

  • Forex (Foreign Exchange): The Forex market is the largest and most liquid market globally, where currencies are traded. This market operates 24 hours a day, 5 days a week, and offers a wide range of opportunities for traders. Forex prices are influenced by economic indicators, central bank policies, and geopolitical events.

  • Cryptocurrencies: Cryptocurrencies, such as Bitcoin and Ethereum, are decentralized digital assets. They are traded on cryptocurrency exchanges and can experience significant price volatility due to speculative trading, regulatory news, and market sentiment. Cryptocurrencies operate 24/7.
  • Commodities: Commodities include raw materials and agricultural products like oil, gold, coffee, and wheat. These are traded on commodity markets and are typically influenced by supply and demand factors. For example, oil prices can fluctuate based on geopolitical events, while agricultural prices are often impacted by weather conditions.

Common Trading Terms

Here are some key terms you’ll frequently encounter when learning about and participating in trading:

  • Leverage: Leverage allows traders to control a large position in the market with a smaller amount of capital. For example, if you use 10x leverage, you can control $10,000 worth of assets with just $1,000. While leverage increases potential profits, it also magnifies the risks of losses.

  • Margin: Margin refers to the amount of capital required to open a leveraged position. It acts as collateral for the leveraged trade. If the trade moves against you, the broker may require additional funds to maintain the position.

  • Spreads: The spread is the difference between the bid price (the price a buyer is willing to pay) and the ask price (the price a seller is asking). In most markets, traders pay the spread when they enter or exit a trade. Tight spreads are favorable for traders as they reduce transaction costs.

  • Order Types: There are different order types to manage trades effectively:
    • Market Order: Executes at the current market price.
    • Limit Order: Executes when the asset reaches a specified price or better.
    • Stop Loss: Automatically closes a trade to limit losses if the price moves against the position.
    • Take Profit: Automatically closes a trade to lock in profits when the price hits a predetermined level.

Fundamental Analysis Basics

Fundamental analysis is a method used to evaluate the value of an asset based on economic, financial, and other qualitative data. It’s crucial for longer-term trading and investing. Fundamental factors such as interest rates, GDP, and company earnings can have a significant impact on asset prices.

  • GDP (Gross Domestic Product): The GDP is one of the most important indicators of a country’s economic health. A growing GDP typically signals a healthy economy and can drive market confidence. Traders watch GDP reports to gauge the strength of a country’s economy.

  • Interest Rates: Central banks set interest rates to control inflation and stimulate economic growth. When interest rates rise, currencies tend to appreciate, and stock prices may fall. Conversely, when interest rates fall, it can make borrowing cheaper, which can stimulate spending and growth.

  • Earnings Reports: Companies release earnings reports quarterly, which show their revenue, profits, and future forecasts. Positive earnings reports can drive stock prices up, while negative reports can cause prices to fall. Traders often look for earnings surprises (when actual results differ significantly from analysts’ expectations).

  • News Events: Global news events, such as elections, natural disasters, or policy changes, can create volatility in the market. News affects investor sentiment, which can lead to sudden market movements. Traders often use news analysis to predict potential price shifts.
